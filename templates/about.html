About Dr APJ Abdul Kalam

Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam (1931–2015) was an Indian aerospace scientist and statesman who served as the 11th President of India from 2002 to 2007. Born in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, he came from a modest background; his early life involved helping his family and pursuing his education in physics and aerospace engineering.

Kalam graduated in physics from St. Joseph's College, Tiruchirappalli, and later studied aerospace engineering at the Madras Institute of Technology. He began his career as a scientist with the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and later joined the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), where he was a key figure in India's civilian space programme and military missile development efforts.

During the 1960s and 1970s Kalam worked on projects related to India's first satellite launch vehicle (SLV-III) and later on systems for ballistic missile development. He played a prominent role in the Integrated Guided Missile Development Program (IGMDP), contributing to the development of missiles such as Agni and Prithvi. These programs established important technical capabilities within India and led to his widespread recognition as a leader in the country's aerospace efforts.

In 1998, following a series of nuclear tests in India, Kalam served as the Chief Scientific Adviser to the Prime Minister and Secretary of the Defence Research and Development Organisation, where he helped coordinate scientific aspects of national defence. Over his career he authored and co-authored technical papers and several books aimed at students, engineers, and the general public.

Kalam was widely respected for his emphasis on education, scientific temper, and youth engagement. After his term as President he continued to work with universities and research institutions, delivering lectures and mentoring students. He received several honours, including India's highest civilian award, the Bharat Ratna.

He passed away in 2015 while delivering a lecture at the Indian Institute of Management, Shillong. His legacy is often described in terms of engineering achievements, public service, and efforts to inspire young people to pursue science and nation-building.
